Essay Writing 40m · 500 words RBI DEPR · IES
▾
Format
Intro (hook + thesis, ~80w) → Body: 3–4 PEEL paragraphs (~300w) → Counterargument (~50w) → Conclusion (synthesis, ~70w). Avoid first person. Title required.
Example Prompt
"Inflation targeting has served as the cornerstone of monetary policy in emerging economies. Critically examine its limitations in the Indian context."
Model Answer
The Limits of Inflation Targeting in India

Inflation targeting, adopted formally by India in 2016 under the Monetary Policy Framework Agreement, represents a paradigm shift from discretionary policymaking to rule-based central banking. While it has anchored inflationary expectations and enhanced the RBI's credibility, the framework carries structural limitations when applied to a complex, supply-driven economy like India.

Supply-side dominance: Indian inflation is largely driven by food and fuel prices — components over which monetary policy has limited traction. A supply shock from a deficient monsoon can push headline CPI well above the 4% target irrespective of the policy rate. Aggressive rate hikes in such episodes suppress demand without addressing the underlying supply constraint, generating stagflationary risk.

Transmission lags: India's financial system remains bank-dominated, with significant segments of the population and small enterprises operating outside formal credit channels. The transmission of repo rate changes to lending rates is slow and incomplete, reducing the precision of inflation targeting as a stabilisation tool.

Growth trade-off: In a developing economy with high unemployment and infrastructure deficits, an unwavering focus on price stability can crowd out investment at critical junctures. The 2019–20 slowdown underlined the tension between meeting a 4% CPI target and supporting productive capacity expansion.

Critics argue for a flexible mandate that explicitly weights output gaps. Yet, abandoning the framework risks undermining hard-won credibility. The resolution lies in complementing inflation targeting with coordinated supply-side policies — strengthening agricultural supply chains, rationalising energy price pass-through, and deepening financial inclusion to improve monetary transmission.

Inflation targeting remains a necessary anchor, but not a sufficient framework. Its limitations in India are not failures of design so much as reminders that price stability and development cannot be separated in an economy still building its structural foundations.
Précis Writing 30m · ~160 words RBI DEPR · UPSC
▾
Format
Compress source (≈500w) to exactly 1/3 length (150–170w). Give a title. Write in third person. No lifted phrases — paraphrase throughout. No personal opinion. Retain all key ideas.
Example Prompt (source passage, abridged)
"Digital public infrastructure has emerged as a transformative force in India's development story. The JAM trinity — Jan Dhan accounts, Aadhaar biometric identity, and Mobile connectivity — has enabled direct benefit transfers reaching over 300 million households... [500-word passage continues]"
Model Précis (~160 words)
Digital Infrastructure as a Development Lever

India's digital public infrastructure has become a pivotal instrument of socioeconomic transformation. The convergence of universal banking access, biometric identification, and widespread mobile connectivity has enabled targeted welfare delivery to hundreds of millions of previously underserved households, dramatically reducing leakages in subsidy programmes.

The infrastructure has also catalysed private innovation. Fintech firms have built payment and credit services on open interoperability layers, expanding formal financial inclusion well beyond the reach of traditional institutions. Small enterprises have gained access to capital through digital credit assessment, accelerating formalisation of the economy.

Nevertheless, structural challenges persist. Rural connectivity gaps, digital literacy deficits, and data privacy concerns limit the framework's reach and raise governance questions. Realising the full potential of digital infrastructure will require sustained public investment in connectivity, robust regulatory oversight, and citizen-centred data protection standards. The gains so far are significant but unevenly distributed.
Reading Comprehension 25m · 5 Qs RBI DEPR · UPSC
▾
Format
Economics-themed passage (~600w). 5 questions (5m each). State your answer directly in the first sentence. Support with 2–3 sentences from the passage. Never import external knowledge. Infer only what the text supports.
Example Question
"According to the passage, what are the two principal constraints on the effectiveness of monetary policy transmission in India?"
Model Answer
The passage identifies bank dominance and financial exclusion as the two principal constraints on monetary policy transmission in India.

The author notes that the majority of credit in India flows through scheduled commercial banks rather than capital markets, creating a single, slow-moving channel for policy rate signals. Transmission is further impeded by the large share of the population and small businesses that operate outside the formal banking system, and therefore do not respond to changes in the policy rate at all.

The passage implies that until financial deepening reduces these structural gaps, interest rate adjustments will remain a blunt instrument — effective at the margin but unable to influence the breadth of economic activity required for reliable stabilisation.
Letter Writing 20m · ~250 words RBI DEPR · IES
▾
Format
Formal letter only. Structure: Sender's address → Date → Recipient's designation + address → Subject line → Salutation (Dear Sir/Madam) → Body (3 paras) → Closing (Yours faithfully / sincerely) → Signature. 1 of 3 options.
Example Prompt
"Write a formal letter to the Chief General Manager, Reserve Bank of India, recommending measures to improve credit access for micro-enterprises in semi-urban areas."
Model Answer
The Senior Research Fellow Institute of Economic Studies New Delhi – 110 001 5 June 2026 The Chief General Manager Department of Regulation Reserve Bank of India Mumbai – 400 001 Subject: Recommendations for Improving Credit Access for Micro-Enterprises in Semi-Urban Areas Dear Sir/Madam, I write to draw attention to the persistent credit gap facing micro-enterprises operating in semi-urban India and to suggest regulatory measures that may address this structural constraint. Despite the expansion of Priority Sector Lending guidelines, field evidence indicates that micro-enterprises in semi-urban clusters face significant barriers to formal credit: absence of collateral, lack of formal accounting records, and limited proximity to bank branches. These constraints have pushed a substantial segment of productive enterprise toward informal moneylenders at significantly higher cost. I respectfully recommend that the Reserve Bank consider: (i) mandating district-level credit flow reporting disaggregated by enterprise size and location, enabling targeted monitoring; (ii) promoting cash-flow-based lending norms for enterprises with verifiable digital transaction histories; and (iii) incentivising co-lending arrangements between scheduled banks and registered NBFC-MFIs with established semi-urban reach. These measures, taken together, could meaningfully extend formal credit to underserved productive segments without compromising prudential standards. I trust these recommendations merit consideration in the Bank's forthcoming review of Priority Sector Lending norms. Yours faithfully, (Signature) Senior Research Fellow
Report Writing 20m · ~300 words IES · UPSC
▾
Format
Official format: To / From / Date / Subject (header block). Then: Objective → Findings (numbered or bulleted) → Analysis → Recommendations → Conclusion. No personal pronouns. Factual, formal tone throughout.
Example Prompt
"You are an officer in the Ministry of Finance. Write a report to the Finance Secretary on the recent trend of rising household indebtedness and its implications for financial stability."
Model Answer
To : Finance Secretary, Ministry of Finance From : Deputy Secretary (Economic Analysis Division) Date : 5 June 2026 Subject : Rising Household Indebtedness — Trends and Financial Stability Implications 1. OBJECTIVE This report examines the recent increase in household debt levels in India and assesses its implications for macroeconomic and financial stability. 2. FINDINGS (i) Household debt-to-GDP ratio has risen from approximately 35% in FY20 to 40% in FY24, driven primarily by consumer credit, personal loans, and housing finance. (ii) NBFC and fintech lending to sub-prime household segments has grown at 28% CAGR over the same period. (iii) Loan-to-income ratios in urban households, particularly for unsecured consumer credit, have risen to levels flagging early stress in RBI's Financial Stability Reports. 3. ANALYSIS While a portion of the increase reflects formalisation of previously informal borrowing, the pace of unsecured retail credit growth raises systemic concern. Rising interest rates have increased debt-service burdens, potentially crowding out household consumption and productive investment. Concentration of stress in lower-income urban segments could translate into elevated NBFC NPAs if employment conditions deteriorate. 4. RECOMMENDATIONS (i) Direct RBI to tighten risk weights on unsecured consumer loans above a defined loan-to-income threshold. (ii) Commission a quarterly household debt survey disaggregated by income decile. (iii) Review macroprudential buffers for NBFC-consumer lenders with retail portfolios growing above 25% annually. 5. CONCLUSION Household indebtedness, while currently manageable in aggregate, warrants proactive monitoring and targeted macroprudential intervention to prevent localised stress from amplifying into systemic risk. Submitted for consideration. (Signature) Deputy Secretary
