Focus on Environmental Valuation, Natural Resource Economics, and Global Frameworks. This is IES General Economics revision for GE-03 (Paper III) at DSE/JNU masters level. Target length: 15 minutes.

Cover these specific sub-concepts: total economic value framework — use value (direct: recreation, food; indirect: flood control, carbon sequestration; option: preserving future use) versus non-use value (existence: value of knowing something exists; bequest: value for future generations); contingent valuation method — willingness to pay surveys, hypothetical bias, scope insensitivity, embedding effect, strategic bias — limitations that make it contested; hedonic pricing method — how property price differentials reveal willingness to pay for environmental quality, wage differentials for risky jobs (hedonic wage equation); travel cost method — using recreation site visits to value non-market benefits; benefit-cost analysis in environmental projects; theory of exhaustible resources — Hotelling's rule: resource net price must rise at the rate of interest, user cost concept, optimal depletion path, efficiency conditions; renewable versus non-renewable resources and sustainable yield; common property resources and the tragedy of the commons — Hardin's open access problem; Ostrom's polycentric governance solution — conditions for successful collective action (small group, clear boundaries, monitoring, graduated sanctions); Joint Forest Management as Indian example; climate change economics — social cost of carbon, carbon pricing rationale, Green Climate Fund structure; green GDP — UN system of environmental and economic accounting (SEEA); Brundtland definition of sustainable development — weak versus strong sustainability; ecological footprint concept; Local Agenda 21 and Rio frameworks.

DO NOT cover: Pigouvian tax, cap and trade, or command-and-control instruments (those are in Audio 1a); industrial economics (Audio 2a/2b); public finance (Audio 3a/3b); state and market planning (Audio 4).

For every valuation concept: make the logic concrete. "The hedonic pricing method works like this: two identical houses in the same neighbourhood differ only in proximity to a polluted river. If the house near the river sells for 5 lakh rupees less, that price gap reveals how much buyers are willing to pay to avoid the pollution — it is the implicit price of environmental quality, read off from actual market transactions."

Structure: Open with the 2025 IES question on contingent valuation limitations and the 2024 question on Hotelling's rule. Build from total economic value through valuation methods to resource economics to global frameworks. End with 3 most likely IES 2026 patterns: (1) compare CVM and hedonic pricing — method, data requirements, and limitations, (2) Hotelling's rule — derive and apply to a non-renewable resource policy question, (3) Ostrom's critique of Hardin — under what conditions can communities self-govern commons.
