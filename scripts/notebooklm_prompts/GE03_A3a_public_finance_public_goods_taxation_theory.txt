Focus on Public Finance — Public Goods, Tax Theory, and Optimal Taxation. This is IES General Economics revision for GE-03 (Paper III) at DSE/JNU masters level. Target length: 15 minutes.

Cover these specific sub-concepts: public goods — non-excludability and non-rivalry as defining properties; pure vs impure public goods; free rider problem and market failure; Samuelson's condition for optimal provision — sum of individual marginal rates of substitution equals marginal cost of production (social marginal benefit equals marginal cost); voluntary provision under-supplies public goods — why; Lindahl pricing — personalised prices for public goods, why it fails in practice (preference revelation problem); merit goods and demerit goods — justification for government provision; theory of taxation — four canons (equity, efficiency, certainty, convenience); ability to pay principle versus benefits principle; horizontal and vertical equity; incidence of taxation — who actually bears the burden: formal incidence (who pays the tax) vs effective incidence (who bears the welfare loss); tax incidence in competitive markets — statutory incidence irrelevant, burden falls on inelastic side; excess burden of taxation (deadweight loss) — graphical derivation as welfare triangle, algebraic formula (half times tax squared times elasticity times quantity); excess burden rises with square of tax rate — implication for rate structure; optimal tax theory — Ramsey rule: to minimise total excess burden for a given revenue requirement, tax rates should reduce quantity demanded by equal proportions across all goods (inverse elasticity rule); Diamond-Mirrlees production efficiency theorem; optimal income tax — Mirrlees model, trade-off between equity and efficiency; marginal effective tax rate and its impact on labour supply.

DO NOT cover: Indian tax structure (GST, income tax in India) — that is GE-04; public debt and Ricardian equivalence (Audio 3b); fiscal federalism and Tiebout model (Audio 3b); environmental taxes in applied context (GE-03 Audio 1a).

For every tax concept: translate the algebra into spoken English. "The Ramsey rule says: to raise a given amount of tax revenue with the least possible efficiency loss, you should reduce the demand for each taxed good by the same percentage. This means taxing necessities — goods with inelastic demand — more heavily. It is efficient but regressive, which creates a direct conflict with equity."
