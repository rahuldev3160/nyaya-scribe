Focus on Industrial Economics — Market Structure, Barriers to Entry, and Firm Conduct. This is IES General Economics revision for GE-03 (Paper III) at DSE/JNU masters level. Target length: 15 minutes.

Cover these specific sub-concepts: Structure-Conduct-Performance paradigm — the causal chain from market structure to firm conduct to performance; critiques of the SCP paradigm (reverse causality, Chicago school critique); market concentration measures — four-firm concentration ratio (CR4), Herfindahl-Hirschman Index calculation (sum of squared market shares), Lerner index (price minus marginal cost over price), Tobin's Q; determinants of market structure — minimum efficient scale, economies of scale relative to market size, product differentiation, sunk costs; barriers to entry — structural barriers (cost advantages, capital requirements, network effects, switching costs) versus strategic barriers created by incumbents; limit pricing — Bain's model: incumbent sets price low enough to make entry unprofitable, trade-off between foregone profit and entry deterrence; Sylos postulate and the entry limit price formula; predatory pricing — pricing below cost to drive out rivals, below-cost pricing tests (Areeda-Turner rule); excess capacity as commitment device for deterrence; contestable markets theory — Baumol's idea that the threat of entry disciplines incumbents even in concentrated markets, perfectly contestable market conditions; how sunk costs make markets non-contestable.

DO NOT cover: natural monopoly regulation, Ramsey pricing, or competition policy (those are in Audio 2b); R&D and innovation (Audio 2b); environmental economics (Audios 1a/1b); public finance or state-market planning.

For applied examples: ground each concept in a real industry. "Minimum efficient scale matters for market structure like this: if the MES of a steel plant requires output equal to 30 percent of total market demand, the market can support at most 3 to 4 efficient firms — the technology itself creates a concentrated oligopoly, not anticompetitive behaviour."

Structure: Open with the 2025 IES question on limit pricing and the 2024 question on contestable markets. Build from concentration measurement through barriers to strategic conduct. End with 3 most likely IES 2026 patterns: (1) HHI calculation and market concentration assessment, (2) limit pricing — Bain's model and Sylos postulate, price vs welfare trade-off, (3) contestable markets — conditions and critique.
