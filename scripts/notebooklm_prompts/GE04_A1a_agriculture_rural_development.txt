Focus exclusively on Agriculture and Rural Development in India. This is IES General Economics revision for GE-04 (Paper IV) at DSE/JNU masters level. Target length: 15 minutes.

Cover these specific sub-concepts: structure of Indian agriculture — land holding patterns (small and marginal farmers dominate), inverse farm size-productivity relationship and its explanations (supervision costs, family labour, credit constraints); green revolution — technological change (HYV seeds, irrigation, fertilisers), institutional dimensions, regional concentration (Punjab, Haryana), environmental costs (soil degradation, water depletion), missing the eastern heartland; agricultural marketing — APMC Acts and their market fragmentation problem, e-NAM as a national electronic platform for unified market, Farmer Producer Organisations as collective bargaining mechanisms, price discovery failures and middlemen margins; Minimum Support Price — rationale (price stabilisation, income support), how MSP is set (CACP recommendations, A2+FL vs C2 cost concepts), effectiveness (only 6 percent of farmers sell at MSP, spatial coverage gaps), WTO constraints: MSP procurement inflates Amber Box subsidy beyond the 10 percent de minimis threshold; MGNREGA — demand-driven employment guarantee, wage rate mechanism, 100-day entitlement, social audit requirement, multiplier effect on rural wages and consumption, implementation gaps (delayed wages, inadequate worksites, fund flow problems); PM Awas Yojana Gramin, PMGSY (rural roads), Swachh Bharat — access to infrastructure as productivity channel; agricultural credit — institutional (NABARD, cooperative banks, RRBs, Kisan Credit Card) versus informal (moneylenders) — interest rate differential and debt trap; land reform history — zamindari abolition, tenancy reforms, ceiling acts — political economy of incomplete implementation.

DO NOT cover: poverty measurement methodology (Audio 1b); labour laws or labour market analysis (Audio 1c); urbanisation and migration (Audio 1c).

For every policy: give the economic rationale AND the implementation reality. "MGNREGA works as an automatic stabiliser — it is demand-driven, so in drought years when agricultural employment collapses, MGNREGA demand surges automatically. The 15-day payment rule is meant to enforce accountability. But in practice, payment delays of 60 to 90 days are common in many states, which defeats the income-support purpose entirely."

Structure: Open with the 2024 IES question on MGNREGA and the 2025 question on MSP and WTO constraints. Build from land structure through green revolution to marketing to credit. End with 3 most likely IES 2026 patterns: (1) MSP — rationale, effectiveness, and WTO Green Box versus Amber Box implications, (2) MGNREGA — design, outcomes, and reforms needed, (3) agricultural marketing reforms — APMC problem and e-NAM solution.
