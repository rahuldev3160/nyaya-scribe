Focus on Public Finance — Public Expenditure, Public Debt, and Fiscal Federalism. This is IES General Economics revision for GE-03 (Paper III) at DSE/JNU masters level. Target length: 15 minutes.

Cover these specific sub-concepts: public expenditure theories — Wagner's Law (as per capita income rises, public spending as share of GDP rises — empirical evidence and critique); Peacock-Wiseman displacement effect (crises permanently ratchet up public spending); Musgrave's three functions of government (allocation, distribution, stabilisation); composition of public expenditure — productive vs unproductive, revenue vs capital; quality of public expenditure debate — capital expenditure has higher multiplier; public debt — internal vs external debt; functional finance vs sound finance views; crowding out hypothesis — loanable funds channel (higher government borrowing raises interest rates and crowds out private investment), asset substitution channel; twin deficits hypothesis — fiscal deficit and current account deficit link; Ricardian equivalence — Barro's argument: rational households fully offset government borrowing by saving more (expecting future taxes), so fiscal policy has no real effect; conditions required for Ricardian equivalence to hold (infinite horizons, perfect capital markets, lump-sum taxes, no liquidity constraints); why Ricardian equivalence fails in practice (credit constraints, finite lives, distortionary taxes, uncertainty); fiscal federalism theory — assignment of functions (Musgrave: stabilisation = centre, redistribution = centre, allocation = local); Tiebout model — "voting with your feet," local jurisdictions compete for residents by offering different tax-expenditure packages; efficient Tiebout equilibrium conditions and its limitations; optimal size of jurisdictions — trade-off between economies of scale and preference heterogeneity; grants-in-aid — matching grants (conditional) vs lump-sum grants (unconditional), flypaper effect (money sticks where it lands).

DO NOT cover: specific Indian fiscal policy (FRBM, GST, Finance Commission) — that is GE-04; optimal tax theory or Ramsey rule (covered in Audio 3a); environmental taxation instruments (GE-03 Audio 1a).

For every debt concept: make the mechanism explicit. "Ricardian equivalence says it does not matter whether the government finances a given level of spending through taxes today or through borrowing today and taxes tomorrow. Rational households see through the veil of public debt — they know the borrowing means higher future taxes, so they save exactly the amount of the tax cut to pay those future taxes. The extra saving neutralises the fiscal stimulus completely."

End with 3 most likely IES 2026 patterns: (1) Ricardian equivalence — conditions, intuition, and three reasons it fails in India, (2) crowding out — explain both channels and evaluate empirical relevance, (3) Tiebout model — assumptions and why it fails in developing countries.
