Focus exclusively on Environmental Externalities and Policy Instruments. This is IES General Economics revision for GE-03 (Paper III) at DSE/JNU masters level. Target length: 15 minutes.

Cover these specific sub-concepts: negative externalities from pollution — the core market failure; social cost versus private cost divergence; Pigouvian tax — derivation of the optimal tax rate equal to marginal external damage, how it internalises costs, information requirements as its main limitation; tradable pollution permits (cap and trade) — how the cap is set, how permits are traded, efficiency properties, comparison with Pigouvian tax under cost uncertainty (Weitzman's analysis: tax preferred when marginal benefit curve is flat, permits when marginal cost curve is flat); command-and-control regulations — uniform standards, technology mandates, and why they are less efficient than market instruments; deposit-refund systems; regulatory failure and political economy of environmental policy; benefit-cost analysis in environmental projects — net present value, discount rate controversy in environmental contexts; Coase theorem applied to environmental externalities — property rights solution, transaction costs as the binding constraint.

DO NOT cover: environmental valuation methods (CVM, hedonic, travel cost — those are in Audio 1b); exhaustible resources or Hotelling's rule (Audio 1b); common property resources or tragedy of the commons (Audio 1b); climate change global frameworks (Audio 1b); industrial economics or public finance.

For every mechanism: speak in plain English. "Cap and trade sets a hard limit on total pollution — the cap. It then issues permits equal to the cap and lets firms trade them. A firm that can cut pollution cheaply will cut beyond its permit requirement and sell the surplus. A firm facing high abatement costs will buy permits. The market price of permits settles where the marginal cost of abatement is equal across all firms — this is exactly the condition for cost efficiency."

Build from the externality problem to Pigouvian solution to permits to command-and-control comparison. End with 3 most likely IES 2026 patterns: (1) compare Pigouvian tax vs cap and trade — efficiency, equity, and information requirements, (2) Weitzman's choice between price and quantity instruments, (3) Coase theorem — state conditions and explain why it fails in practice.
