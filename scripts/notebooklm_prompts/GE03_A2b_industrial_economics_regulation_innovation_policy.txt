Focus on Industrial Economics — Regulation of Natural Monopoly, Innovation, and Competition Policy. This is IES General Economics revision for GE-03 (Paper III) at DSE/JNU masters level. Target length: 15 minutes.

Cover these specific sub-concepts: natural monopoly — definition (subadditive cost function), declining average cost, why competitive market fails; regulation options for natural monopoly: marginal cost pricing (efficient but causes losses requiring subsidy), average cost pricing (breaks even but creates deadweight loss), Ramsey pricing (inverse elasticity rule: mark up inversely proportional to price elasticity — minimises DWL subject to budget constraint), rate-of-return regulation (Averch-Johnson effect: over-capitalisation when allowed rate of return exceeds cost of capital), price cap regulation (RPI-X formula); public enterprises — rationale for state ownership, performance criteria, X-inefficiency problem (Leibenstein), disinvestment and privatisation rationale; R&D and innovation — Schumpeterian hypothesis: large firms with market power innovate more because they can appropriate returns (monopoly rents fund R&D); empirical evidence for and against the Schumpeter hypothesis; Arrow's alternative view: competitive firms have stronger incentive to innovate (replacement effect); patent system — rationale (dynamic incentive) vs cost (static monopoly distortion), patent length and breadth trade-off; competition policy objectives — consumer welfare vs total welfare standard; merger types and anti-competitive effects; horizontal merger analysis — SSNIP test; vertical restraints — resale price maintenance, exclusive dealing, tying.

DO NOT cover: market structure measurement or barriers to entry (covered in Audio 2a); environmental economics; public finance theory (Audio 3a/3b); state and market planning (Audio 4).

For every regulatory concept: explain the economic mechanism. "Rate-of-return regulation tells the firm: your allowed profit cannot exceed a set percentage of your capital base. The Averch-Johnson effect shows this creates a perverse incentive — if the allowed rate of return is higher than the firm's actual cost of capital, the firm can boost allowed profits by inflating its capital base. So it over-invests in capital relative to what minimises cost."

Structure: Open with the 2025 IES question on Ramsey pricing and the 2025 question on the Schumpeter hypothesis. Spend the most time on natural monopoly regulation since IES tests it almost every year. End with 3 most likely IES 2026 patterns: (1) compare four methods of natural monopoly regulation — efficiency, equity, and incentive effects, (2) Schumpeter vs Arrow on market structure and innovation — which view does evidence support, (3) patent system trade-off — design the optimal patent policy.
